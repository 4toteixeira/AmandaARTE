Anima/Animus and the Rebis Within


This body of work is rooted in Carl Jung’s depth psychology and the ancient symbolism of alchemy. At its core, it explores the Anima and Animus: the feminine and masculine archetypes that exist within all individuals, regardless of gender. Jung saw these internal figures as essential for psychological balance: the Anima represents emotion, intuition, and relational sensitivity, often repressed in men; the Animus, on the other hand, embodies reason, assertiveness, and structure, frequently suppressed in women. When one denies either force, the psyche becomes fragmented.

To visualize this inner duality, I turn to the alchemical symbol of the Rebis, a mythical being composed of both male and female aspects in a single body. The Rebis, often shown crowned and holding symbols of both the sun and moon, stands for the alchemical marriage, or the union of opposites. For alchemists, this fusion wasn’t just symbolic, but a path toward spiritual enlightenment and wholeness. In my interpretation, the Rebis becomes a transgender or gender-fluid figure,beyond binaries, seeking the divine not in separation, but in integration.

Through charcoal drawing, layers of veladura, and varnish, I work with the physical dualities of light and shadow, using gradients to represent the spectrum between masculine and feminine energies. The figures emerge not from absolutes, but from in-between states—ambiguous, reflective, evolving. These gradients echo how gender and identity truly function: as fluid continua rather than rigid categories.

In today’s world, where gender debates are increasingly polarized, this inner dialogue between Anima and Animus feels more urgent than ever. My work suggests that healing lies not in choosing sides, but in listening inward, honoring both the sun and the moon within. By recognizing and integrating our own inner opposites, we come closer to the Rebis, a being whole unto itself.

What does she remember of me?: This body of work is a visual and emotional investigation into the slow unraveling of memory through the lens of my grandmother’s experience with Alzheimer’s disease. It is both a personal archive and a poetic inquiry into forgetting, silence, and the instability of identity.

The paintings, objects, and installations that compose this series are constructed through processes of accumulation and erasure, echoing the cyclical nature of Alzheimer’s: remembering, losing, and sometimes inventing. Layers of acrylic, oil, and textile are applied and removed, leaving traces, gaps, and blurred forms. I often work with family photographs, written fragments, and recorded audio from conversations with my grandmother before and after her diagnosis, using them not as documentation, but as materials of transformation.

This is not a portrait of a disease. Rather, it is a meditation on memory as a material, something that can dissolve, become transparent, or take new form through time. In doing so, I reflect on what it means to care, to witness, and to carry someone’s story when they can no longer tell it.

I am particularly interested in how memory loss alters our perception of the body and time. In some works, I use textile and embroidery to mirror the repetitive gestures of caregiving. In others, video and sound are used to evoke confusion, silence, or the absence of response. The domestic space, especially the table, the bed, and the photograph frame, recurs throughout the work as a quiet but charged stage.

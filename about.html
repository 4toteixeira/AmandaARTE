the Artist

I make art to hold space for what has been forgotten, misinterpreted, or quietly transforming. My work lives in the threshold: between memory and erasure, structure and vulnerability, ideology and intimacy. I see art as a practice of listening, witnessing, and reconstructing meaning through emotional, historical, and material excavation. Over the past 8 years, I have immersed myself in a solitary process of making and study; mastering traditional oil painting techniques while deepening my research into architecture as memory and the visual systems that shape identity. I am particularly drawn to ideological architectures such as Soviet modernism, Brutalism, and the Bauhaus, movements that once promised utopia, but now stand as hollow monuments to unrealized futures. These forms serve as metaphors for psychic states: abandoned ideals, fractured selves, and the fragile residue of cultural projection. At the heart of my practice is a desire to understand how cultural narratives shape personal memory, and how liminal spaces, whether physical ruins or emotional absences, can hold profound symbolic power. I engage with the archetypal language of the unconscious, inspired by Jungian psychology and the alchemical figure of the Rebis, to explore inner dualities: feminine/masculine, soft/harsh, rational/instinctive. These dualities inform not just my subjects, but my material language: layered oil glazes, muted palettes, distressed surfaces, and moments of intentional incompletion. The Remai Modern’s focus on critical, contemporary discourse and the Cité internationale des arts’ international artistic ecosystem offer a unique opportunity to situate this inquiry within a broader global context. I am especially interested in the dialogue between Saskatchewan’s landscapes and colonial histories, and Europe’s post-war architectural ideologies, how both geographies hold silent structures that inform belonging, erasure, and transformation. In this residency, I do not seek answers but resonance. I aim to produce works that are not declarative but reflective—visual texts that invite viewers to question how space, memory, and ideology live within them. This is not simply about buildings or symbols. It is about the way our internal architecture is shaped by the external world, and how, through art, we might begin to see those structures anew.
